DriftDown

Binaural sound to ease your brain toward sleep.

Headphones required. Binaural beats only work with stereo headphones or earbuds — each ear needs to receive a slightly different tone.

This is a sound tool, not a medical treatment. Evidence on binaural beats for sleep is promising but mixed — effects vary a lot from person to person. If you have epilepsy, a pacemaker, or a diagnosed sleep disorder, check with a doctor before use. Not for use while driving or operating machinery.

The actual science

A healthy brain drifting off naturally cycles from relaxed alpha waves (~8–12 Hz) into theta (~4–8 Hz) and then delta (~0.5–4 Hz) as sleep deepens. In insomnia, that transition is often weaker or slower to arrive.

Binaural beats are created by playing two slightly different tones, one per ear. Your brain perceives the mathematical difference between them as a slow pulsing "beat" — and some studies suggest brain activity can partially entrain toward that beat frequency, nudging it toward theta/delta ranges associated with sleep onset.

This app generates those tones live using the Web Audio API rather than a compressed audio file, so nothing gets re-encoded or lossy on the way to your ears.

Being direct about the evidence: results are mixed. Some trials show meaningfully faster sleep onset and better sleep quality; others show little difference versus calming music alone. It seems to help a meaningful share of people, but it isn't a guaranteed fix, and it's not a substitute for treating an underlying sleep disorder.

Sources: Sleep Foundation — Binaural Beats for Sleep, Meta-analysis: acoustic stimulation for insomnia (PMC, 2025), All-night binaural beat entrainment study protocol (Frontiers/PMC), Theta binaural beats & theta power in primary insomnia (PubMed)
